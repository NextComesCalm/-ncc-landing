What's inside the kit

19 pages, designed to be used together. Print once, use for every trip.

Visual Story

8 illustrated panels: getting ready to leave, arriving at the store, getting a cart, walking through the aisles, finding things on the list, the checkout line, paying, and going to the car.

Parent Scripts

5 ready phrases: telling them the plan before you go, giving them a job, when they ask for something you're not getting, for the checkout wait, and for the end.

Step Schedule Cards

12 illustrated cards showing what happens in order — from leaving home to loading the car. Bring them along and let your child follow the steps.

Sensory Expectations

What to see (bright lights, lots of people, full shelves), hear (music, announcements, beeping tills), and feel (cold in the freezer section) — framed as preparation, not a list of worries.

Brave Choices

8 coping options including having a job on the list, counting items, and breathing when it gets busy — small choices that give your child a sense of control in a big, loud space.

Custom Cards + More

Blank cards, the "no" plan (what to do when you can't have something), a comfort plan, and a debrief — so the trip ends on solid ground.

Who this kit is for

This kit is for parents who dread grocery trips because their child finds them genuinely hard — the noise, the lights, the "no" that comes out of nowhere.

It works especially well for children who find busy, loud, unpredictable environments overwhelming, and for children who need to know in advance what the rules are for a given trip. That includes children who are naturally cautious, children who need more preparation than most, and children who find sensory-heavy spaces hard to move through calmly.

You don't need a specific diagnosis for this to help. Most children under 8 do better when they know what's coming. This kit gives you a clear, warm way to tell them — before you walk in the door.

Common questions

When should I use this kit?

Read the visual story the night before or the morning of a grocery trip. The "no" plan cards are most useful — review those before you walk in so your child knows what to expect when the answer is no.

Is this kit helpful for children with sensory sensitivities?

Yes — the sensory expectations page walks through the specific sounds, lights, and sensations of a grocery store, which helps sensory-sensitive children feel less surprised by the environment. Many parents of neurodivergent children find this page the most useful part. Educational support, not medical advice.

What about the "I want that" moments?

The kit includes a "no plan" section — a simple, gentle script for before the trip that helps children know in advance what the rules are. It doesn't eliminate the hard moment, but it means the child heard the boundaries before they hit them. Educational support, not medical advice.
