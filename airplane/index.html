What's inside the kit

19 pages, designed to be used together. Print once, read together before the trip.

Visual Story

8 illustrated panels: packing the bag at home, driving to the airport, checking in and getting boarding passes, going through security, waiting at the gate, boarding and finding your seat, fastening the seatbelt and takeoff, landing and arriving.

Parent Scripts

5 ready-to-say phrases: describing security before you get there, for the wait at the gate, for takeoff (explaining the sounds and the tilting), if ears pop during ascent or descent, and for landing and arrival.

Step Schedule Cards

12 illustrated cards covering every step from home to destination. Pull them out at the airport so your child can see exactly where they are in the journey.

Sensory Expectations

What your child will see (the busy airport, the plane interior, clouds from the window), hear (PA announcements, jet engine sounds during takeoff, the cabin quieting at altitude), and feel (the seatbelt, the tilting during takeoff and landing, possible ear pressure) — framed as expected and manageable.

Brave Choices

8 coping options your child can practise at home: yawning or swallowing during ear pressure, having a comfort item on the plane, counting to ten during takeoff sounds, looking out the window, and more.

Custom Cards + More

Blank cards for flight-specific details (airline name, seat number, who they'll see at the destination), a comfort plan, and debrief questions for after you land.

How to use it

1

Print at home

Download the PDF and print on US Letter paper. No special paper or printer required.

2

Read together

Sit down with your child 1–3 nights before the flight. Read through the visual story. Talk about the brave choices. Repeat if helpful.

3

Arrive ready

Nothing about the airport or the plane will come as a surprise — your child has already been through every step in the story, including the sounds and the feelings.

Who this kit is for

This kit is for parents whose child is about to fly for the first time — or whose child has found previous flights hard. It works especially well for children who need to know what will happen before they experience it: what security involves, what the sounds of takeoff mean, and what to do when their ears feel funny.

That includes children who find unfamiliar environments overwhelming and children who need extra preparation before any new experience. You don't need a specific reason to use it. Most children under 8 benefit from knowing exactly what is coming before they walk into a busy airport for the first time.

Common questions

When should I use this kit?

Read the visual story 1–3 nights before the flight. The sensory expectations page — especially the section on ear pressure — is worth going through more than once so your child has a plan ready when it happens in the moment.

Does the kit explain ear pressure — and what to do about it?

Yes. The sensory expectations page describes what ear pressure feels like and why it happens, in language appropriate for young children. The brave choices include yawning, swallowing, and chewing (if your child chews gum) as simple ways to help. Educational support, not medical advice.

Is this kit helpful for children who find airports particularly overwhelming?

Yes — airports are one of the most sensory-dense environments a young child encounters: loud, crowded, constantly changing, with unfamiliar rules. The visual story walks through every part of the airport experience, so nothing is new on the day. Many parents of neurodivergent children find this the most useful part of the kit. Educational support, not medical advice.
