What's inside the kit

19 pages, designed to be used together. Print once, use for every appointment.

Visual Story

8 illustrated panels walking through a real doctor visit — arriving at the clinic, checking in at the desk, the waiting room, the nurse calling your name, being weighed and measured, the doctor checking ears, eyes, throat, and reflexes, the doctor explaining what they found, and going home.

Parent Scripts

5 ready-to-say phrases for the moments that are hardest to navigate — for the waiting room, when the nurse calls your name, for the stethoscope, if there might be a needle, and for the end of the visit.

Step Schedule Cards

12 illustrated cards showing each step of the visit. Take them in your bag. Pull them out in the waiting room so your child can follow along and see what comes next.

Sensory Expectations

A parent-facing page covering what your child will see (the exam table with paper, the instruments the doctor uses), hear (the click and light of the otoscope, the doctor's voice), and feel (the cool stethoscope, the tongue depressor, gentle pressure during the exam) — framed as reassuring preparation.

Brave Choices

Practical coping options your child can practise at home — squeezing a parent's hand, counting ceiling tiles, focusing on a spot on the wall, and taking a slow deep breath when something feels hard.

Custom Cards + More

Blank cards to fill in appointment-specific details, space to write your comfort plan, and debrief questions for after the visit.

Who this kit is for

This kit is for parents who want to reduce resistance at the doctor's office — before the appointment even starts.

It works especially well for children who find new environments and unfamiliar procedures hard to manage, and for children who need to know what to expect in advance — including whether there might be a needle. The visual story covers real instruments and real interactions, so nothing inside the exam room is genuinely new. That same predictability makes this kit useful for sensory-sensitive and neurodivergent children as well.

You don't need a child with specific challenges for this to help. Most children under 8 benefit from knowing what's coming. This kit gives you a warm, clear way to tell them.

Common questions

When should I use this kit with my child?

Read the visual story together 1–3 nights before the appointment. Repeat it 2–3 times if your child finds doctor visits particularly worrying. The step cards can go in your bag and come out in the waiting room.

What if there might be a needle at the appointment?

Be honest. The kit includes guidance on how to tell your child that there might be a needle, using "if" framing — "if the doctor thinks you need one." Honesty builds more trust than a surprise, and most children do better when they've had a chance to think it through in advance. Educational support, not medical advice.

Is this kit helpful for children who need extra preparation?

Yes. The kit was designed with predictability as its core principle. The visual story covers real exam instruments and real interactions — the stethoscope, the otoscope, the tongue depressor — so nothing at the appointment is truly unfamiliar. Many parents of sensory-sensitive and neurodivergent children find the sensory expectations page and parent scripts the most useful parts. Educational support, not medical advice.
