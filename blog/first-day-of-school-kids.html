Preparing Your Child for the First Day of School

For children ages 3–7 · 6 min read

The first day of school is one of the biggest transitions in early childhood — not because of anything dramatic that happens there, but because of everything that's unknown. A new room. A new adult in charge. A long stretch of hours without their familiar people. Children who need to know what happens next before they can feel ready face a day where almost everything is new all at once.

The children who walk in with the most ease on the first day are not always the most outgoing or the most experienced in group settings. They are the children who already know, in some concrete way, what to expect when they walk through the door.

What makes the first day hard

Young children organise their world around familiarity and predictability. A first day of school contains a high concentration of unfamiliars: a new space, a teacher whose name they might know but whose face may still feel strange, other children who are also uncertain, rules that haven't been explained yet, and a goodbye moment that many children don't have words for.

  • They don't know the physical space. Where is the bathroom? Where do they put their bag? Where do they sit?
  • They don't know how the goodbye will work. Will you stay? How long? When will you come back?
  • They don't know the other children. Even children who are socially comfortable can find a room full of strangers overwhelming in the first hours.
  • They don't know what "school" will feel like. The concept is abstract until they have direct experience. The stories, pictures, and walkthroughs you provide before the first day are the only preparation they have.

How to prepare at home

1. Walk through the day in story form

The most effective preparation for a first-day child is a step-by-step walk-through of what will happen. We wake up and have breakfast. We get dressed and pack the bag. We drive to school. We walk in together. We find the coat hook with your name on it. We find your seat. Your teacher says good morning. Then I give you a hug, and I leave, and I come back at pick-up time.

Read this story the night before, and once more in the morning. Children who have heard the sequence of the day already are not encountering it for the first time when they're standing at the classroom door.

2. Make the goodbye plan concrete

The goodbye is the moment most parents and children find hardest. The uncertainty about how it will go often contributes more to first-day difficulty than anything that happens afterward. Make a specific goodbye plan before you go: "I'm going to give you a big hug, we do our secret handshake, and then I leave. I'll be back at the end of the day, after lunch and snack and outside time."

Once you have agreed on the plan, follow it. Do not extend the goodbye in response to distress — it delays the settling that comes after, and teaches the child that more distress produces more goodbye. A warm, certain goodbye is kinder than a long, uncertain one.

3. Visit the space before the first day if possible

Many schools offer orientation visits or allow a brief walkthrough before school begins. If yours does, use it. Walking into a familiar physical space — even once, even briefly — changes the way a child's brain registers the first morning. The classroom is not entirely unknown. They have already been there.

4. Give them one thing to look forward to

Not a bribe — an anchor. Something specific they can hold onto during the part of the day that feels uncertain. "Your teacher told me you're going to build in the block area today" or "you get to eat lunch from your new lunchbox — you get to choose what goes in it tonight." A small, concrete thing that is theirs gives children a thread to follow through an unfamiliar day.

5. Name the reunion clearly

"I will pick you up after school. Right after school, I will be there." Say it more than once. Young children's sense of time is not reliable — "I'll be there at three o'clock" is less meaningful than "I'll be there right after your teacher calls for home time, and we'll walk to the car together." A concrete, visual image of the reunion is more reassuring than a time they can't picture.

If the first morning is hard

Children who cry at drop-off or cling at the classroom door are not broken, and this is not a sign that something went wrong. Separation at the start of school is a normal, well-documented part of early childhood, and for many children it is time-limited — the tears last minutes after the parent leaves, not hours.

What helps most in the moment: a warm, certain goodbye (not hurried or cold, but clear), a brief check-in with the teacher, and trust that your child will find their footing once the familiar beginning is over. Most children who cry at drop-off are settled and engaged by the time circle time begins.

If difficulty persists past the first week or two, a conversation with the teacher about what is happening during the day — not just at drop-off — is worth having.

Common questions

How far in advance should I start preparing my child?

For most children, 3–5 days of simple, low-key preparation is enough — walking through the day in story form, visiting the school if possible, talking about the goodbye plan. Starting too early (weeks ahead) gives worry more time to build without giving the child anything they can act on. The few days before the first day are the most useful window.

My child says they don't want to go. Should I insist?

Yes — calmly and warmly, school is something you go to. What helps is acknowledging the feeling ("I know it feels big") without treating the reluctance as a valid reason to stay home. Children often say they don't want to go about things they genuinely enjoy once they're there. The day is new; reluctance is normal. Name the plan clearly and follow through.

Is it okay to let my child bring a comfort item to school?

If the school allows it, a small comfort item — a photo, a soft toy that lives in the bag, a small object from home — can provide meaningful support for a child navigating a new environment. Check with the teacher about their policy, and talk with your child about when the item lives in the bag vs. when it can come out. Educational support, not medical advice.
