Waiting and Turn Taking: Why It's So Hard for Young Children

For children ages 2–7 · 5 min read

Waiting in line at the post office. Taking turns on the scooter. Sitting in a waiting room with nothing to do while time moves slowly. For adults, waiting is unremarkable. For a child under seven, waiting is one of the hardest things asked of them — not because they are impatient or poorly behaved, but because the brain structures that make waiting manageable are still being built.

Understanding this doesn't make the waiting room quieter, but it does change how you approach it — and there are things that genuinely help children wait longer and take turns more willingly.

Why waiting is genuinely hard at this age

Waiting requires holding a future state in mind ("my turn is coming") while tolerating a present state that is unsatisfying ("I don't have the thing yet"). That's a sophisticated cognitive task. It requires working memory, impulse control, and the ability to delay gratification — all functions that are in active development until roughly age seven, and that develop at different rates in different children.

A two-year-old has almost none of these capacities in place. A five-year-old has some but not reliably. A seven-year-old has significantly more, but will still struggle when waiting is long, the reward is uncertain, or the environment is overstimulating.

This is why telling a young child "just wait" — with no additional structure — rarely works. "Just wait" requires the child to draw on capacities they don't yet reliably have.

What helps in the waiting room

1. Make the wait visible

Adults know time is passing because we have clocks, internal references, and a sense of duration built from years of experience. Young children have almost none of this. "It won't be long" is meaningless. A visual timer — a simple sand timer, or an app that shows the time shrinking — transforms abstract waiting into something the child can watch. When a child can see the wait, they can hold on through it.

2. Give them a job

A child with something to do waits better than a child who is simply waiting. The job doesn't need to be complex or educational. "Your job while we wait is to find all the red things in this room" or "see how many times you can fold this paper." A defined task gives the child's attention somewhere to go. Empty waiting — sitting with nothing — is the hardest kind.

3. Count down with them

Counting gives children a sense of agency over a passive situation. "We're going to count to twenty while we wait for the nurse to call us." They participate in the passing of time rather than simply enduring it. Most children can hold still for a counted wait that would be impossible as an open-ended one.

4. Name the sequence

Children wait better when they know what they're waiting for and what comes next. "We wait here, then the nurse calls us, then we go in and the doctor has a look, then we get our sticker and go home." The sequence transforms the wait from an uncertain limbo into a defined step in a known story. Children who know what they're waiting for are significantly less likely to escalate than children sitting in an undefined pause.

What helps with turn-taking

1. Make the turn concrete and close

Abstract "your turn is coming" is harder to hold onto than "three more turns and then it's yours." Count the turns if you can. "Jack's turn. Mia's turn. Then yours." A child who can see their turn approaching has something to count toward.

2. Give them something to do while they wait for their turn

The hardest part of turn-taking is not the turn itself — it's the watching while someone else has it. Give the waiting child a small physical thing to do: hold the next piece, carry the next card, be in charge of counting the turns. Being assigned a role keeps their hands and attention occupied.

3. Acknowledge how hard the wait feels

"I know it's hard to wait. It feels like a long time." Not to solve it or fix it — just to name it. Children who feel that their experience is understood are slightly less driven to escalate it. The acknowledgement is not agreement that they should have the turn now. It's a recognition that the feeling is real and reasonable for their age.

When the wait is simply too long

There is a real capacity limit, and sometimes the wait is simply longer than a young child can hold. Medical waiting rooms, airport queues, restaurant waits — these are environments designed for adults. Children don't fail at waiting in these places; the wait exceeds their developmental capacity.

The most effective thing in these situations is not better technique — it is reducing the demand. A snack at the start of a restaurant wait, a bag of specific activities for a medical appointment, leaving an airport security queue for a minute if you can. Working within the child's actual capacity, not just the one you wish they had.

And some waiting simply has to happen, regardless of capacity. In those moments, staying calm and present beside a child who is struggling is the most useful thing. Your steady, non-urgent presence is more regulating than any technique.

Common questions

Is it okay to use a screen while waiting?

Yes. A screen in a waiting room or at a restaurant while food arrives is a practical tool, not a parenting failure. The goal is to complete the wait without everyone being miserable — and if a video does that, it does that. You can also use screens selectively: not immediately, but as a "when we've tried other things" option, which gives other strategies a chance to work first.

My child hits or grabs when they can't have a turn. What should I do?

Physically stopping the behaviour — calmly, without anger — and naming what is happening: "It's not your turn yet. Hitting doesn't get you a turn faster." Then give them something to do while they wait, and return to the turn sequence. Don't skip their turn as a consequence — that removes the reward they were working toward and teaches nothing useful. Educational support, not medical advice.

Will my child just get better at waiting as they get older?

Yes — the brain structures that support waiting continue developing through middle childhood and into adolescence. With practice and consistent scaffolding, most children's waiting capacity increases substantially between ages 3 and 7. Children who practise waiting with adult support develop the skill faster than those who are simply told to wait without structure.
